Assets - What You Own

üîí Critical Rule: Assets CANNOT have negative balances

Why? Assets represent resources your business owns. You cannot own negative money, negative inventory, or negative equipment.

Common Asset Accounts:

  • Cash - Money in hand or bank
  • Accounts Receivable - Money owed to you by customers
  • Inventory - Products held for sale
  • Equipment - Tools, machinery, computers
  • Buildings - Real estate owned

Normal Balance: Debit (Positive)

Assets increase with DEBITS and decrease with CREDITS.

‚úÖ Example 1: Owner Invests Cash
Account Debit Credit Balance After
Cash (Asset) $50,000 - $50,000 ‚úÖ
Owner's Capital (Equity) - $50,000 -$50,000

Result: Cash increased from $0 to $50,000. Valid! ‚úÖ

‚úÖ Example 2: Purchase Equipment with Cash
Account Debit Credit Balance After
Equipment (Asset) $10,000 - $10,000 ‚úÖ
Cash (Asset) - $10,000 $40,000 ‚úÖ

Result: Equipment increased, Cash decreased. Both remain positive. Valid! ‚úÖ

‚ùå VIOLATION: Withdrawing More Cash Than Available
Account Current Balance Change Would Result In
Cash (Asset) $5,000 -$10,000 $-5,000 ‚ùå

Problem: You're trying to withdraw $10,000 when you only have $5,000. This would create negative cash, which is impossible!

System Action: üö® Transaction BLOCKED - Integrity violation detected

üí° Real-World Scenario: Running a Coffee Shop

Day 1: You invest $50,000 to start your coffee shop.

Cash (Asset) = $50,000 ‚úÖ Owner's Capital (Equity) = $50,000

Day 2: You buy an espresso machine for $5,000.

Equipment (Asset) = $5,000 ‚úÖ Cash (Asset) = $45,000 ‚úÖ

Day 3: You try to buy inventory for $50,000 with only $45,000 cash.

‚ùå BLOCKED! Cash would become: $45,000 - $50,000 = $-5,000 System Message: "Asset accounts cannot have negative balances!"

üí° Pro Tip

Think of Assets like your wallet. You can't spend money you don't have. If the system warns you about negative asset balance, it means you need more capital (investment) or need to take a loan (liability) first.

Liabilities - What You Owe

üîí Critical Rule: Liabilities CANNOT have positive balances

Why? Liabilities represent debts your business owes. A positive liability would mean you're owed money on a debt, which is logically impossible. Liabilities are naturally negative (you owe money).

Common Liability Accounts:

  • Accounts Payable - Money you owe to suppliers
  • Loans Payable - Bank loans, mortgages
  • Credit Cards Payable - Business credit card debt
  • Salaries Payable - Unpaid employee wages
  • Taxes Payable - Taxes owed to government

Normal Balance: Credit (Negative)

Liabilities increase with CREDITS and decrease with DEBITS.

‚úÖ Example: Taking a Bank Loan
Account Debit Credit Balance After
Cash (Asset) $100,000 - $100,000 ‚úÖ
Loan Payable (Liability) - $100,000 -$100,000 ‚úÖ

Result: You received $100,000 cash (asset up) and now owe $100,000 (liability up). Valid! ‚úÖ

‚úÖ Example: Paying Off Part of the Loan

Current State: Loan Payable = -$100,000

Account Debit Credit Balance After
Loan Payable (Liability) $30,000 - -$70,000 ‚úÖ
Cash (Asset) - $30,000 $70,000 ‚úÖ

Result: Loan decreased from -$100,000 to -$70,000. You still owe $70,000. Valid! ‚úÖ

‚ùå VIOLATION: Paying More Than You Owe

Current State: Loan Payable = -$50,000

Account Current Balance Change Would Result In
Loan Payable (Liability) -$50,000 +$60,000 $10,000 ‚ùå

Problem: You're trying to pay $60,000 on a $50,000 loan. The result would be positive $10,000, which means the bank owes YOU money. Impossible!

System Action: üö® Transaction BLOCKED - Liability cannot have positive balance

üí° Real-World Scenario: Business Loan

Month 1: You take a $100,000 business loan.

Cash (Asset) = $100,000 ‚úÖ Loan Payable (Liability) = -$100,000 ‚úÖ

Month 2-11: You pay $9,000/month for 10 months = $90,000 total.

Loan Payable = -$100,000 + $90,000 = -$10,000 ‚úÖ (Still owe $10,000)

Month 12: Final payment - you accidentally try to pay $15,000.

‚ùå BLOCKED! Loan would become: -$10,000 + $15,000 = $5,000 System Message: "Liability accounts cannot have positive balances!"

Correct Action: Pay exactly $10,000 (or less) to bring loan to $0.

Equity - Owner's Stake

‚úÖ Special Rule: Equity CAN have negative balances

Why? Equity represents the owner's stake in the business. If the owner withdraws more money than they invested, equity can go negative. This means the owner actually owes money back to the company (rare but valid).

‚ö†Ô∏è Important: Positive Equity (Admin Approval Required)

While equity can go positive (owner owes company), this is a rare and serious scenario. Due to its significance, any transaction that would result in positive equity requires administrator approval before it can be posted.

Why? This protects both the business and the user from accidental financial errors in uncommon situations.

Common Equity Accounts:

  • Owner's Capital - Owner's initial investment
  • Retained Earnings - Accumulated profits kept in business
  • Owner's Drawings - Money withdrawn by owner
  • Common Stock - For corporations

Normal Balance: Credit (Negative)

Equity increases with CREDITS and decreases with DEBITS.

‚úÖ Example: Owner Invests Money
Account Debit Credit Balance After
Cash (Asset) $50,000 - $50,000
Owner's Capital (Equity) - $50,000 -$50,000 ‚úÖ

Result: Owner invested $50,000. They now own $50,000 worth of the business. Valid! ‚úÖ

‚úÖ Example: Owner Withdraws Money (Normal)

Current State: Owner's Capital = -$50,000

Account Debit Credit Balance After
Owner's Capital (Equity) $10,000 - -$40,000 ‚úÖ
Cash (Asset) - $10,000 $40,000

Result: Owner withdrew $10,000. They now own $40,000 worth of business. Valid! ‚úÖ

‚úÖ Example: Owner Withdraws MORE Than Invested (RARE but ALLOWED)

Current State: Owner's Capital = -$50,000 (owner invested $50k)

Account Current Balance Change Would Result In
Owner's Capital (Equity) -$50,000 +$60,000 $10,000 ‚úÖ ALLOWED!

Result: Owner withdrew $60,000 when they only invested $50,000. Equity is now POSITIVE $10,000.

Meaning: Owner now owes the company $10,000. This is rare but valid! ‚úÖ

üí° Real-World Scenario: Growing Business

Year 1: Owner invests $100,000.

Owner's Capital = -$100,000 (Owner owns $100k worth of business)

Year 2: Business makes $50,000 profit (retained).

Owner's Capital = -$100,000 Retained Earnings = -$50,000 Total Equity = -$150,000 (Business is worth $150k to owner)

Year 3: Owner withdraws $75,000 for personal use.

Owner's Capital = -$100,000 + $75,000 = -$25,000 ‚úÖ (Still valid - owner still has $25k stake)

Year 4: Owner withdraws another $100,000.

Owner's Capital = -$25,000 + $100,000 = $75,000 ‚úÖ ALLOWED! (Positive equity! Owner now owes company $75k)

Revenue - What You Earn

üîí Critical Rule: Revenue CANNOT have positive balances

Why? Revenue represents income earned. It's naturally negative (credit balance). A positive revenue would mean negative income, which doesn't make sense.

Common Revenue Accounts:

  • Sales Revenue - Income from selling products
  • Service Revenue - Income from providing services
  • Interest Income - Interest earned on deposits
  • Rental Income - Income from renting property

Normal Balance: Credit (Negative)

Revenue increases with CREDITS and decreases with DEBITS.

‚úÖ Example: Making a Sale
Account Debit Credit Balance After
Cash (Asset) $5,000 - $5,000
Sales Revenue (Revenue) - $5,000 -$5,000 ‚úÖ

Result: You made a $5,000 sale. Cash increased, revenue increased. Valid! ‚úÖ

‚ùå VIOLATION: Reversing More Revenue Than Earned

Current State: Sales Revenue = -$10,000

Account Current Balance Change Would Result In
Sales Revenue (Revenue) -$10,000 +$15,000 $5,000 ‚ùå

Problem: You're trying to reverse $15,000 of revenue when you only earned $10,000. This would create positive revenue (negative income). Impossible!

System Action: üö® Transaction BLOCKED - Revenue cannot have positive balance

üí° Real-World Scenario: Coffee Shop Sales

Cash = $1,000 ‚úÖ Sales Revenue = -$1,000 ‚úÖ

‚ùå BLOCKED! Revenue would become: -$950 + $2,000 = $1,050 System Message: "Revenue accounts cannot have positive balances!"

Expenses - What You Spend

üîí Critical Rule: Expenses CANNOT have negative balances

Why? Expenses represent costs incurred. They're naturally positive (debit balance). A negative expense would mean getting money back beyond what you spent, which should be recorded as revenue instead.

Common Expense Accounts:

  • Rent Expense - Office/store rent
  • Salaries Expense - Employee wages
  • Utilities Expense - Electricity, water, internet
  • Supplies Expense - Office supplies, materials
  • Advertising Expense - Marketing costs

Normal Balance: Debit (Positive)

Expenses increase with DEBITS and decrease with CREDITS.

‚úÖ Example: Paying Rent
Account Debit Credit Balance After
Rent Expense (Expense) $2,000 - $2,000 ‚úÖ
Cash (Asset) - $2,000 $48,000

Result: You paid $2,000 rent. Expense increased, cash decreased. Valid! ‚úÖ

‚ùå VIOLATION: Reversing More Expense Than Recorded

Current State: Rent Expense = $10,000

Account Current Balance Change Would Result In
Rent Expense (Expense) $10,000 -$15,000 $-5,000 ‚ùå

Problem: You're trying to reverse $15,000 of expense when you only recorded $10,000. This would create negative expense. Impossible!

System Action: üö® Transaction BLOCKED - Expense cannot have negative balance

üìä Double-Entry Bookkeeping

Every transaction affects AT LEAST two accounts. This is the foundation of modern accounting.

‚öñÔ∏è The Golden Rule: Debits MUST Equal Credits

In every transaction: ‚àë Debits = ‚àë Credits

If this holds true, the accounting equation (Assets = Liabilities + Equity) automatically balances.

The Debit-Credit Rules:

Account Type Increase With Decrease With Normal Balance
Assets DEBIT CREDIT Debit (Positive)
Liabilities CREDIT DEBIT Credit (Negative)
Equity CREDIT DEBIT Credit (Negative)
Revenue CREDIT DEBIT Credit (Negative)
Expenses DEBIT CREDIT Debit (Positive)

üîí System Integrity Rules

Our system enforces these rules to maintain financial integrity and prevent accounting errors.

What Gets Validated:

  1. Double-Entry Balance: Total debits MUST equal total credits in every transaction
  2. Account Balance Rules: Assets, Liabilities, Revenue, Expenses cannot violate balance rules
  3. Accounting Equation: Assets = Liabilities + Equity MUST always be true
  4. Duplicate Accounts: Same account cannot be used twice in one transaction
  5. Posted Transaction Immutability: Posted transactions cannot be edited or deleted

When Validation Happens:

  • Frontend (Real-time): Before you click "Save" - Immediate feedback
  • Backend (Final Check): When saving to database - Cannot be bypassed
  • Post-Transaction: After posting - Validates equation still balances
